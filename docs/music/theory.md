# Theory Cheatsheet

The stuff I look up over and over. Kept short on purpose. Links back to the
[practice log](guitar.md).

## Notes & intervals

The twelve notes, ascending: C, C♯, D, D♯, E, F, F♯, G, G♯, A, A♯, B, then back
to C. A **semitone** is one fret; a **tone** is two.

### Intervals

| Interval | Semitones | Example from C |
|---|---|---|
| Minor 2nd | 1 | C → D♭ |
| Major 2nd | 2 | C → D |
| Minor 3rd | 3 | C → E♭ |
| Major 3rd | 4 | C → E |
| Perfect 4th | 5 | C → F |
| Perfect 5th | 7 | C → G |
| Octave | 12 | C → C |

## Major scale formula

Whole–whole–half–whole–whole–whole–half (T-T-S-T-T-T-S). In C that's all the
white keys: C D E F G A B.

## Chord construction

Triads stack thirds off the scale:

| Chord | Formula | C example |
|---|---|---|
| Major | 1 – 3 – 5 | C E G |
| Minor | 1 – ♭3 – 5 | C E♭ G |
| Diminished | 1 – ♭3 – ♭5 | C E♭ G♭ |
| Dominant 7 | 1 – 3 – 5 – ♭7 | C E G B♭ |

## Keys in songs

Most pop/rock lives in a handful of guitar-friendly keys: G, C, D, A, E. The
**capo** lets you play familiar open shapes in other keys.

### Transposing

Capo on fret **N** raises everything by **N semitones**. So G-shape chords with a
capo on 2 actually sound in A. When a singer says "it's too low," capo up until it
isn't.

## Circle of fifths (the short version)

Going clockwise adds a sharp each step: C → G → D → A → E → B → F♯. Going
counter-clockwise adds flats: C → F → B♭ → E♭. Neighbors on the circle share most
of their notes, which is why those chord changes sound natural.
